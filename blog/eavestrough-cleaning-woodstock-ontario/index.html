Oxford County's Dirty Secret: Clogged Eavestroughs Are Destroying Foundations

Woodstock is the county seat of Oxford County — and with a strong agricultural and residential community stretching from Ingersoll to Tillsonburg, homes throughout this region share a common problem: massive deciduous tree coverage that clogs eavestrough systems every single fall.

When eavestroughs fail, rainwater stops being directed away from your home's foundation. Instead, it pools against your basement walls, saturates the surrounding soil, and slowly but catastrophically undermines your home's structural integrity. In Woodstock's clay-heavy soil, foundation leaks from failed gutters are one of the most common and expensive home repair scenarios.

Professional eavestrough cleaning near Woodstock is not maintenance — it is insurance.

Signs Your Woodstock Home's Eavestroughs Need Immediate Attention

1. Water Spilling Over the Edge During Rain

This is the clearest signal of a complete blockage. The downspout cannot drain fast enough — or at all — because leaves, seed pods from maple trees, and roof grit have formed a solid plug. Every rainstorm then sends a waterfall directly over the channel edge and straight down against your foundation.

2. Sagging or Pulling Away from Fascia

Accumulated debris is heavier than most homeowners realize. Wet leaves can weigh 10–15 lbs per linear foot inside a gutter channel. This weight gradually pulls the hanger brackets from the fascia board, creating a gap where water tracks directly behind the channel and rots the underlying wood.

3. Rust Staining on Siding or Foundation

Orange or brown streaks running down your siding from eavestrough seams indicate the standing water inside has compromised the channel seams. This requires immediate re-sealing or section replacement before the channel fails entirely.

4. Plant Growth in Gutters

If you can see grass, moss, or small plants growing from your eavestrough, the system has been clogged long enough for organic matter to take root. At this stage, simple cleaning is required along with a full inspection for seam separation and hanger damage.

How often should Woodstock homes clean their eavestroughs?

At minimum, twice per year — once in late spring after seed pods fall, and once in early November after maximum leaf drop. Homes with heavy tree coverage directly overhead may need 3–4 cleanings annually.
